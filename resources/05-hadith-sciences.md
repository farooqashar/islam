# Hadith Sciences

## What is Hadith

A **hadith** (plural: ahadith) is a report of the sayings, actions, approvals, or descriptions of Prophet Muhammad. The **Sunnah** is the broader practice and way of life of the Prophet, while hadith are the textual records that document it.

Each hadith has two parts:

- **Isnad** (chain of narration): the list of transmitters going back to the Prophet
- **Matn** (text): the actual content of the report

**Hadith Qudsi** (Sacred Hadith) are words of Allah conveyed by the Prophet in his own wording, distinct from the Quran which is Allah's exact words in both meaning and wording.

## Classification by Authenticity

- **Sahih** (authentic): continuous chain of upright, precise narrators, no irregularity or hidden defect
- **Hasan** (good): meets all conditions but with slightly lesser precision in a narrator
- **Da'if** (weak): fails one or more conditions - subtypes include mursal (missing Companion link), munqati (broken chain), mu'dal (two+ consecutive missing links), mudallas (concealed defect), mawdu (fabricated)
- **Mawdu** (fabricated): proven to be invented, often with a liar in the chain

## Classification by Number of Transmitters

- **Mutawatir**: transmitted by so many narrators at every level that fabrication is impossible. Produces certainty. Example: the description of how to pray
- **Ahad** (solitary): does not reach mutawatir level. Subdivided into:
  - **Mashhur** (well-known): three or more chains
  - **Aziz** (rare): at least two chains at every level
  - **Gharib** (strange): only one chain at some level

## Classification by Reference

- **Marfu** (elevated): attributed to the Prophet
- **Mawquf** (stopped): attributed to a Companion
- **Maqtu** (cut): attributed to a Tabi'i (successor)

## Hadith Authentication Methodology

The five conditions for a sahih hadith (per Ibn al-Salah):

1. Continuous chain (Ittisal al-Sanad)
2. Uprightness of each narrator (Adalah)
3. Precision of each narrator (Dabt)
4. No irregularity (absence of Shudhudh)
5. No hidden defect (absence of Illah)

**Ilm al-Rijal** (science of narrator evaluation) involves biographical dictionaries documenting each narrator's reliability, memory, and character. **Al-Jarh wa al-Ta'dil** is the formal system of criticizing or approving narrators.

## The Six Major Collections (Kutub al-Sittah)

### Sahih al-Bukhari

- **Compiler**: Muhammad ibn Ismail al-Bukhari (d. 256 AH / 870 CE)
- **Size**: approximately 7,275 hadith with repetitions (~2,602 unique)
- **Method**: strictest criteria of any collection; took 16 years to compile
- **Status**: considered the most authentic book after the Quran by Sunni scholars

### Sahih Muslim

- **Compiler**: Muslim ibn al-Hajjaj (d. 261 AH / 875 CE)
- **Size**: approximately 7,563 hadith with repetitions (~3,033 unique)
- **Method**: known for superior arrangement; groups all chains of a hadith together
- **Status**: second most authentic collection

### Sunan Abu Dawud

- **Compiler**: Abu Dawud al-Sijistani (d. 275 AH / 889 CE)
- **Size**: approximately 5,274 hadith
- **Focus**: legal hadith relevant to fiqh rulings

### Jami al-Tirmidhi

- **Compiler**: al-Tirmidhi (d. 279 AH / 892 CE)
- **Size**: approximately 3,956 hadith
- **Unique feature**: grades each hadith and notes which jurists use it

### Sunan al-Nasa'i

- **Compiler**: al-Nasa'i (d. 303 AH / 915 CE)
- **Size**: approximately 5,758 hadith
- **Method**: stringent narrator criteria, sometimes considered stricter than Muslim

### Sunan Ibn Majah

- **Compiler**: Ibn Majah (d. 273 AH / 887 CE)
- **Size**: approximately 4,341 hadith
- **Note**: its inclusion in the six is contested; some scholars place Muwatta Malik or Sunan al-Darimi in its place

## Other Important Collections

- **Muwatta Imam Malik** (d. 179 AH / 795 CE): earliest major compilation, blends hadith with Medinan practice
- **Musnad Ahmad ibn Hanbal** (d. 241 AH / 855 CE): approximately 30,000 hadith arranged by narrator
- **Sahih Ibn Hibban** and **Sahih Ibn Khuzaymah**: additional sahih collections
- **al-Mustadrak** by al-Hakim (d. 405 AH): hadith meeting Bukhari/Muslim criteria but not in their books
- **Riyad al-Salihin** by al-Nawawi (d. 676 AH / 1277 CE): thematic collection for everyday guidance
- **al-Arba'in al-Nawawiyyah** (Forty Hadith of al-Nawawi): 42 foundational hadith covering core teachings
- **Bulugh al-Maram** by Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani: hadith used as evidence in fiqh rulings

## Key Hadith Scholars

- **Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani** (d. 852 AH / 1449 CE): authored Fath al-Bari, the premier commentary on Sahih al-Bukhari
- **Al-Nawawi** (d. 676 AH / 1277 CE): authored the commentary on Sahih Muslim and Riyad al-Salihin
- **Al-Dhahabi** (d. 748 AH / 1348 CE): authored Mizan al-I'tidal on narrator evaluation
- **Ibn al-Salah** (d. 643 AH / 1245 CE): authored the Muqaddimah, systematizing hadith sciences
- **Al-Albani** (d. 1420 AH / 1999 CE): controversial modern scholar who re-graded thousands of hadith

## Famous Individual Hadith

**Hadith of Jibril**: Angel Gabriel came in human form and asked the Prophet about Islam (submission), Iman (faith), and Ihsan (excellence) - defining all three levels of the religion. (Sahih Muslim 8)

**Actions are by intentions**: "Actions are judged by intentions, and each person will get what they intended." (Sahih al-Bukhari 1, Sahih Muslim 1907)

**Love for your brother**: "None of you truly believes until he loves for his brother what he loves for himself." (Sahih al-Bukhari 13, Sahih Muslim 45)

**Leave doubt**: "Leave that which makes you doubt for that which does not make you doubt." (al-Tirmidhi 2518, al-Nasa'i 5711)

**Perfection of Islam**: "Part of the perfection of a person's Islam is leaving that which does not concern him." (al-Tirmidhi 2317)

## Modern Hadith Studies

Digital databases like **sunnah.com** and **shamela.ws** have made hadith searchable and accessible worldwide. Contemporary debates include the re-grading work of al-Albani, the role of hadith in modern fatwa-making, and Western academic approaches to hadith (the works of Schacht, Motzki, and Jonathan Brown).
