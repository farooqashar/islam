# Sufism (Tasawwuf)

## What is Tasawwuf

**Tasawwuf** (Sufism) is the inner, spiritual dimension of Islam. It is rooted in the concept of **Ihsan** (excellence in worship) from the Hadith of Jibril: "To worship Allah as though you see Him, and if you cannot see Him, then indeed He sees you."

Sufism is often described through three layers:

- **Shariah**: the outward law
- **Tariqah**: the spiritual path
- **Haqiqah**: the inner truth/reality

## Historical Development

### Early Ascetics (1st-2nd Century AH)

- **Hasan al-Basri** (d. 110 AH / 728 CE): renowned for his piety and sermons on fear of God
- **Rabia al-Adawiyya** (d. 185 AH / 801 CE): pioneered the concept of selfless divine love
- **Ibrahim ibn Adham** (d. ~777 CE): prince-turned-ascetic

### Formative Period (3rd-4th Century AH)

- **Dhul-Nun al-Misri** (d. 245 AH / 859 CE): introduced the concept of ma'rifa (gnosis)
- **Bayazid Bastami** (d. 261 AH / 874 CE): "ecstatic" Sufism, concept of fana
- **Al-Junayd al-Baghdadi** (d. 298 AH / 910 CE): championed "sober" Sufism grounded in Shariah
- **Mansur al-Hallaj** (d. 309 AH / 922 CE): executed for declaring "Ana al-Haqq" ("I am the Truth")

### Classical Period

- **Al-Ghazali** (d. 505 AH / 1111 CE): his **Ihya Ulum al-Din** reconciled Sufism with mainstream Sunni orthodoxy

## Key Sufi Concepts

- **Nafs** stages: Nafs al-Ammara (commanding), Nafs al-Lawwama (self-reproaching), Nafs al-Mutma'inna (tranquil)
- **Maqamat** (stations): Tawba, Sabr, Zuhd, Tawakkul, Rida
- **Ahwal** (states): Khawf, Raja, Mahabbah, Shawq, Uns, Fana
- **Fana** (annihilation of ego in God) and **Baqa** (subsistence through God)
- **Dhikr** (remembrance): the central Sufi practice
- **Wilayah**: sainthood; the **Awliya Allah** (friends of God)
- **Sama**: spiritual audition (music and poetry as devotional practice)

## Major Sufi Orders (Tariqas)

| Order | Founder | Origin | Known For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Qadiriyyah** | Abdul Qadir al-Jilani (d. 1166) | Baghdad | Oldest and most widespread |
| **Naqshbandiyyah** | Baha al-Din Naqshband (d. 1389) | Central Asia | Silent dhikr, sobriety |
| **Chishtiyyah** | Moinuddin Chishti (d. 1236) | South Asia | Love, Qawwali music |
| **Shadhiliyyah** | Abu al-Hasan al-Shadhili (d. 1258) | North Africa | Gratitude, intellectual depth |
| **Mevlevi** | Jalal al-Din Rumi (d. 1273) | Anatolia | Whirling dervishes, poetry |
| **Tijaniyyah** | Ahmad al-Tijani (d. 1815) | North/West Africa | Widespread in sub-Saharan Africa |

## Great Sufi Figures and Poets

- **Jalal al-Din Rumi** (1207-1273): author of the Masnavi, one of the most widely read poets in the world
- **Ibn Arabi** (1165-1240): formulated Wahdat al-Wujud (Unity of Being)
- **Farid al-Din Attar** (d. ~1221): Conference of the Birds
- **Hafiz of Shiraz** (1315-1390): pinnacle of Persian lyric poetry

## Sufism and Islamic Orthodoxy

- **Al-Ghazali** successfully argued that Sufism was essential to a complete Islamic life
- **Ibn Taymiyyah** critiqued specific Sufi practices while accepting core Tasawwuf
- The **Wahhabi/Salafi** movement broadly opposes organized Sufism
- **Historical reality**: most classical Sunni scholars practiced some form of Tasawwuf

## Sufism in the Modern World

- Sufi orders remain strong in Turkey, South Asia, West Africa, Southeast Asia, and the Balkans
- **Music traditions**: Qawwali (South Asia), Mevlevi whirling (Turkey), Gnawa (Morocco)
- Rumi is one of the best-selling poets in the West
- Shrine pilgrimage (ziyarah) remains a major devotional practice for millions worldwide
