# Islamic Jurisprudence (Fiqh)

## Definition

**Fiqh** literally means "deep understanding." In Islamic scholarship, it refers to the human understanding and application of divine law. It is distinct from **Shariah**, which is the divine law itself as revealed in the Quran and Sunnah. Fiqh is the scholarly effort to derive practical rulings from those sources.

Major categories of Fiqh:

- **Ibadat**: worship (prayer, fasting, zakat, hajj)
- **Mu'amalat**: transactions and commercial law
- **Munakahat**: family law (marriage, divorce, custody)
- **Jinayat**: criminal law
- **Siyar**: international relations and laws of war

## Usul al-Fiqh (Principles of Jurisprudence)

### Primary Sources

1. **Quran**: the first and highest source of law
2. **Sunnah**: the Prophet's practice as recorded in hadith
3. **Ijma** (consensus): agreement of qualified scholars on a ruling
4. **Qiyas** (analogical reasoning): extending a known ruling to a new case sharing the same effective cause (illah)

### Secondary Sources

- **Istihsan**: juristic preference, departing from strict analogy for a better outcome
- **Maslaha Mursalah**: public interest not directly addressed by text
- **Istishab**: presumption of continuity (things remain as they were until proven otherwise)
- **Urf**: local custom, accepted where it does not contradict text
- **Sadd al-Dhara'i**: blocking the means to harm (preventive rulings)

## The Five Legal Rulings (al-Ahkam al-Khamsah)

Every human action falls into one of five categories:

| Ruling | Arabic | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Obligatory | Wajib / Fard | Required; rewarded for doing, sinful for leaving |
| Recommended | Mandub / Mustahabb | Rewarded for doing, no sin for leaving |
| Permissible | Mubah | No reward or sin either way |
| Disliked | Makruh | Better to avoid, no sin for doing |
| Prohibited | Haram | Sinful to do, rewarded for avoiding |

**Fard Ayn** is an individual obligation (e.g., five daily prayers). **Fard Kifayah** is a communal obligation fulfilled if enough people perform it (e.g., funeral prayer, seeking knowledge in certain fields).

## The Four Sunni Schools (Madhahib)

### Hanafi School

- **Founder**: Abu Hanifa al-Nu'man (699-767 CE)
- **Method**: extensive use of Qiyas and Ra'y (reasoned opinion), Istihsan
- **Spread**: Turkey, Central Asia, South Asia, parts of the Arab world, the Balkans
- **Key scholars**: Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani, al-Sarakhsi
- **Note**: largest school by number of followers; was the official school of the Ottoman Empire

### Maliki School

- **Founder**: Malik ibn Anas (711-795 CE)
- **Method**: emphasis on the practice of the people of Medina (Amal Ahl al-Madinah) as a source, strong use of Maslaha
- **Spread**: North Africa, West Africa, parts of the Gulf (UAE, Bahrain, Kuwait)
- **Note**: the Muwatta is both a hadith collection and a fiqh manual

### Shafi'i School

- **Founder**: Muhammad ibn Idris al-Shafi'i (767-820 CE)
- **Method**: systematized Usul al-Fiqh in his landmark work al-Risalah; balanced use of text and analogy
- **Spread**: East Africa, Southeast Asia (Indonesia, Malaysia), Egypt, Yemen, parts of the Levant
- **Note**: al-Shafi'i is often called the "father of Islamic jurisprudence" for formalizing legal methodology

### Hanbali School

- **Founder**: Ahmad ibn Hanbal (780-855 CE)
- **Method**: strict adherence to Quran and Sunnah, minimal use of Qiyas, reliance on hadith even if weak over analogy
- **Spread**: Saudi Arabia, Qatar
- **Note**: smallest of the four schools; later influenced the Wahhabi/Salafi movement

## Shi'a Jurisprudence (Ja'fari School)

- Named after Imam Ja'far al-Sadiq (702-765 CE)
- Uses **Aql** (reason) as a formal source alongside Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma
- Relies on hadith from the Prophet and the Twelve Imams
- The **Usuli** school (dominant) allows living scholars to perform Ijtihad; the **Akhbari** school restricts rulings to transmitted texts
- **Marja al-Taqlid** (source of emulation): a senior scholar whom laypeople follow
- Predominant in Iran, Iraq, Lebanon, Bahrain, Azerbaijan

## Maqasid al-Shariah (Objectives of Islamic Law)

Developed most fully by **al-Shatibi** (d. 1388), this framework identifies the higher objectives behind Islamic rulings:

### The Five Necessities (Daruriyyat)

1. **Preservation of Religion** (Din)
2. **Preservation of Life** (Nafs)
3. **Preservation of Intellect** (Aql)
4. **Preservation of Lineage/Family** (Nasl)
5. **Preservation of Wealth** (Mal)

Below these are **Hajiyyat** (needs that ease hardship) and **Tahsiniyyat** (refinements and embellishments).

## Ijtihad and Taqlid

- **Ijtihad**: independent legal reasoning by a qualified scholar (Mujtahid)
- **Taqlid**: following a qualified scholar's rulings without necessarily knowing the evidence
- The historical debate on whether the "gates of Ijtihad" were closed after the classical period
- Modern revival: most contemporary scholars advocate for renewed Ijtihad within established methodology

## Modern Islamic Law

- **Codification**: the Ottoman Mecelle (1869-1876) codified Hanafi civil law; modern Muslim-majority states have various personal status codes
- **Islamic banking and finance**: governed by Shariah boards; avoids riba (interest), gharar (excessive uncertainty), and maysir (gambling)
- **Contemporary fatwa institutions**: Dar al-Ifta (Egypt), the Islamic Fiqh Academy (OIC), European Council for Fatwa and Research
- **Emerging issues**: bioethics (organ transplant, IVF, genetic engineering), digital transactions, cryptocurrency, environmental fiqh
