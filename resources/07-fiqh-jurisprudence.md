# Fiqh: Islamic Jurisprudence and Law

> "For every people there is a direction to which they turn; so vie with one another in good deeds." -- Quran 2:148

Fiqh is the science of Islamic jurisprudence—the understanding and application of Islamic law derived from the Quran, Sunnah, Ijma' (consensus), and Qiyas (analogical reasoning). It addresses both worship practices and worldly transactions.

## The Four Sources of Islamic Law (Usul al-Fiqh)

### 1. The Quran (Al-Kitab)

The primary source containing explicit rulings and principles. About 500 verses (out of 6,236) directly address legal matters.

**Categories of Quranic rulings**:
- **Explicit commands**: "Establish prayer" (2:43)
- **Prohibitions**: "Forbidden to you is carrion" (5:3)
- **Conditions and qualifications**: Requirements for valid acts
- **Principles**: General maxims for deriving new rulings

### 2. The Sunnah (Prophetic Tradition)

The teachings, practices, and approvals of the Prophet Muhammad, preserved in hadith collections.

**Types of Sunnah**:
- **Sunnah Fi'liyyah**: The Prophet's actions (how he performed prayer, fasted, etc.)
- **Sunnah Qawliyyah**: His verbal statements and rulings
- **Sunnah Taqririyyah**: His silent approval of companions' actions

**Major Hadith Collections**:
- **Sahih al-Bukhari**: Most authentic collection (~7,500 hadith)
- **Sahih Muslim**: Second most authentic (~7,500 hadith)
- **Sunan Abu Dawud, An-Nasa'i, At-Tirmidhi, Ibn Majah**: Four other major collections
- **Musnad Ahmad**: Largest collection (~40,000 hadith with repetitions)

### 3. Ijma' (Consensus)

The agreement of qualified Islamic scholars on a legal ruling after the Prophet's death.

**Types of Ijma'**:
- **Sarih (Explicit)**: Scholars explicitly state their agreement
- **Sukuti (Silent)**: Scholars know of a ruling and don't object
- **Qat'i (Certain)**: Consensus on definite matters
- **Dhanni (Probable)**: Consensus on matters with room for interpretation

**Historical examples**:
- Stoning an adulterer who is married
- Prohibition of selling wine, even to non-Muslims
- Punishment for apostasy from Islam
- Obligation of compulsory education

### 4. Qiyas (Analogical Reasoning)

Applying the ruling of a Quranic or Sunnah ruling to a new case that shares the same reason ('illah).

**Structure**: 
1. The known case (al-asl) with a known ruling
2. The new case (al-far')
3. The common reason ('illah)
4. The deduced ruling

**Example**: The Quran forbids wine (khamr). Using Qiyas, scholars extended this to other intoxicating substances sharing the same 'illah (intoxication).

## The Schools of Islamic Law (Madhabs)

After the Prophet's death, different methodologies emerged. Four Sunni schools are widely accepted as orthodox:

### 1. Hanafi School (Abu Hanifah, d. 150 AH/767 CE)

- **Founder**: An-Nu'man ibn Thabit Abu Hanifah
- **Region**: Prevalent in Central Asia, Turkey, Egypt, Levant, Pakistan, Afghanistan, parts of China
- **Followers**: ~30-40% of Muslims
- **Characteristics**: 
  - Extensive use of Qiyas (analogical reasoning)
  - Accepts Istihsan (juristic preference)
  - Permits use of reason within Islamic bounds
  - Flexible and accommodating to changing circumstances
- **Key works**: Mukhtasar al-Quduri, Hidayah

### 2. Maliki School (Malik ibn Anas, d. 179 AH/795 CE)

- **Founder**: Malik ibn Anas
- **Region**: Prevalent in North Africa, West Africa, parts of Middle East, Spain (historically)
- **Followers**: ~15-25% of Muslims
- **Characteristics**:
  - Heavy reliance on the Sunnah of Medina
  - Accepts Maslaha Mursalah (public interest)
  - Emphasizes preservation of religion and welfare
  - Conservative in certain areas, flexible in others
- **Key works**: Muwatta (earliest hadith collection with legal analysis)

### 3. Shafi'i School (Muhammad ibn Idris al-Shafi'i, d. 204 AH/820 CE)

- **Founder**: Muhammad al-Shafi'i
- **Region**: Southeast Asia, Egypt, parts of Middle East, Levant, East Africa
- **Followers**: ~25-35% of Muslims
- **Characteristics**:
  - Systematized Usul al-Fiqh (principles of jurisprudence)
  - Balanced approach between Quran and Sunnah
  - Strict in some areas (marriage, divorce), flexible in others
  - Influential in Islamic legal theory
- **Key works**: Ar-Risalah (foundational jurisprudence text), Al-Umm

### 4. Hanbali School (Ahmad ibn Hanbal, d. 241 AH/855 CE)

- **Founder**: Ahmad ibn Hanbal
- **Region**: Saudi Arabia and Gulf states, parts of Middle East
- **Followers**: ~5-10% of Muslims
- **Characteristics**:
  - Strict adherence to Quran and Sunnah
  - Limited use of Qiyas; prefers explicit texts
  - Rejects many rational arguments
  - Emphasis on literal interpretation
  - Conservative in lifestyle matters
- **Key works**: Al-Musnad (largest hadith collection of the period)

### Other Recognized Schools

- **Jafari School**: Followed by Twelver Shias; prevalent in Iran and parts of Iraq
- **Zaidi School**: Followed by Zaidis; historically in Yemen
- **Ismaili School**: Followed by Ismaili Shias; various branches

## Categories of Legal Rulings (Ahkam)

Islamic law classifies all human actions:

1. **Wajib (Obligatory)**: Commanded by Sharia; neglecting it is sinful
   - Example: The five daily prayers

2. **Mandub (Recommended)**: Encouraged but not obligatory; rewarded if done, no sin if omitted
   - Example: Extra voluntary prayers (Sunnah)

3. **Haram (Forbidden)**: Prohibited by Sharia; performing it is sinful
   - Example: Consuming pork, wine, stealing

4. **Makruh (Disliked)**: Discouraged but not forbidden; discouraged but not sinful if done
   - Example: Excessive use of perfume while fasting

5. **Mubah (Permissible)**: Neither commanded nor forbidden; neutral in Islamic law
   - Example: Eating when not fasting

## Major Areas of Islamic Law

### 'Ibadah (Worship)

- **Tawhid**: Monotheism and its practices
- **Salah**: The five daily prayers with conditions, times, invalidators
- **Zakat**: Obligatory charity on wealth
- **Sawm**: Fasting during Ramadan with exemptions
- **Hajj**: Pilgrimage to Mecca and its rituals
- **Adhan**: The call to prayer and its regulations

### Mu'amalah (Transactions)

#### Family Law
- **Marriage (Nikah)**: Conditions for valid marriage, mahr (bridal gift), consent, witnesses
- **Divorce (Talaq)**: Types, procedures, waiting period ('idda), maintenance (nafaqah)
- **Inheritance**: Distribution of property after death according to fixed shares
- **Guardianship**: Rights and responsibilities of parents and guardians
- **Maintenance**: Support obligations between family members

#### Contract and Commercial Law
- **Sale (Bay')**: Conditions of valid sales, price, goods, consent
- **Partnership (Shirkah)**: Types and rules of business partnerships
- **Agency (Wakalah)**: Representation and delegation of authority
- **Gift (Hibah)**: Transfer of property without exchange
- **Lease (Ijara)**: Rental and hiring agreements
- **Mortgages and Collateral**: Security for loans

#### Monetary and Financial Transactions
- **Riba (Usury/Interest)**: Strictly prohibited in all forms
- **Murabaha**: Cost-plus sale financing (Islamic alternative to loans)
- **Musharaka**: Profit-sharing partnerships
- **Islamic Banking**: Modern implementations of Sharia-compliant finance

### Jinayah (Criminal Law)

#### Hudud (Prescribed Punishments)

Fixed penalties for specific crimes established in the Quran:

- **Murder and Assault**: Retaliation (Qisas) or compensation (Diyyah)
- **Theft (Sariqah)**: Hand amputation (extreme conditions must be met)
- **Adultery (Zina)**: Stoning if married, flogging if unmarried
- **Theft of property**: Restitution and possible imprisonment
- **Rebellion (Hiraba)**: Armed robbery and highway assault; severe penalties
- **Slander (Qadhf)**: False accusation of adultery; 80 lashes
- **Apostasy (Riddah)**: Renouncing Islam; capital punishment (contested by modern scholars)
- **Drinking alcohol (Shurb)**: Flogging (40 to 80 lashes)

#### Ta'zir (Discretionary Punishment)

Punishments determined by judges for crimes without prescribed penalties:
- Prison sentences
- Fines
- Public censure
- Temporary exile

#### Qisas wa-Diyyah (Retaliation and Compensation)

- **Retaliation**: "A life for a life" for intentional murder
- **Compensation**: Payment to heirs if victim's family forgives
- **Accidental killing**: Guaranteed diyyah payment
- **Injury**: Proportionate retaliation or compensation

### Siyasah (Governance)

- **Rules of state leadership**: The Caliph's duties and limitations
- **Shura (Consultation)**: Democratic principles in Islamic governance
- **Taxation**: Jizya (non-Muslim tax), Kharaj (land tax), Ushr (agricultural tax)
- **War and Peace**: Conditions for jihad, treaties, prisoner treatment
- **Administration**: Court systems, police, public welfare

## Principles of Islamic Jurisprudence (Qawa'id al-Fiqhiyyah)

### The Five Greater Principles

1. **"Matters are judged by their intentions"** (Al-Umur bimaqasidiha)
   - An action's ruling depends on the actor's intention

2. **"Certainty is not dispelled by doubt"** (Al-Yaqin la yazul bil-shak)
   - What is established as certain is not changed by doubt

3. **"Harm must be removed"** (Ad-Darar yuzal)
   - Islamic law prioritizes removing harm

4. **"Hardship brings about facility"** (Al-Mashaqqah tajlib at-taysir)
   - Difficulty leads to ease and exemptions

5. **"Custom is a basis for ruling"** (Al-'Adah muhakkamah)
   - Local customs shape how laws are applied

### Other Important Principles

- **"Prohibition of blocking means"**: An act is forbidden if it leads to forbidden results
- **"The lesser harm is tolerated for the greater benefit"**: Sacrificing small interests for major welfare
- **"What is forbidden cannot be made permissible by necessity"**: Except in life-threatening situations
- **"Public interest supersedes individual interest"** (Maslaha)

## Methodology of Legal Reasoning (Ijtihad)

**Ijtihad** is the exertion of intellectual effort to derive Islamic legal rulings. It requires:

### Requirements for a Mujtahid (Legal Scholar)

1. **Quranic expertise**: Deep knowledge of the Quran, asbab an-nuzul, nasikh wa-mansukh
2. **Hadith sciences**: Understanding which hadith are authentic and weak
3. **Classical language**: Mastery of Arabic grammar and meanings
4. **Jurisprudence**: Study of previous rulings and methodologies
5. **Principles of jurisprudence (Usul)**: How to derive rulings
6. **Piety and fear of God**: Moral uprightness to avoid error and bias

### Types of Ijtihad

- **Mutlaq (Absolute)**: Independent reasoning without restriction to a particular school
- **Muqayyad (Restricted)**: Reasoning within the framework of an established school
- **Taqlid (Imitation)**: Following a scholar or school without independent reasoning (accepted for non-scholars)

### Contemporary Ijtihad

Modern scholars address issues unforeseen in classical times:
- Internet and digital transactions
- Modern medicine and bioethics
- Climate change and environmental law
- Organ donation and transplantation
- Space law and exploration

## Development of Islamic Law

### Early Period (1st-2nd AH / 7th-8th CE)

- Direct reliance on Prophet's rulings
- Expansion into new territories with new legal questions
- Emergence of different methodologies

### Classical Period (2nd-5th AH / 8th-11th CE)

- Systematization of jurisprudence
- Establishment of the four main schools
- Compilation of hadith collections
- Development of usul al-fiqh

### Medieval Period (5th-13th AH / 11th-19th CE)

- Refinement of school doctrines
- Production of exhaustive legal commentaries
- "Gates of Ijtihad" considered closed; taqlid emphasized
- Schools became institutionalized

### Modern Period (13th AH onwards)

- Reassessment of jurisprudential principles
- Contemporary Ijtihad addressing new issues
- Comparative study with other legal systems
- Integration of modern governance structures

## Islamic Legal Maxims (Qawaid)

Islamic jurists have condensed complex rulings into memorable maxims:

- "Five things have no diya (compensation): pen, teeth, whip, plants, and walls"
- "Necessity permits the forbidden" (with exceptions for core beliefs)
- "Doubt in validity invalidates" (in worship)
- "The Muslim's hand is up until he fulfills his promise"
- "Ownership is not transferred except by delivery"

## Challenges and Debates in Modern Fiqh

### Traditional vs. Contemporary

- **Issue**: How much can classical rulings change for modern contexts?
- **Positions**: Conservative scholars defend tradition; reformists seek adaptation
- **Example**: Women's roles in education, work, and leadership

### Ijtihad vs. Taqlid

- **Issue**: Should modern Muslims practice independent reasoning or follow established schools?
- **Positions**: Some promote Ijtihad as obligatory; others defend school loyalty
- **Example**: Should Muslims have flexibility in ritual practices for modernity?

### Maslaha (Public Interest)

- **Issue**: To what extent can Islamic law be modified for public welfare?
- **Positions**: Conservative scholars limit maslaha; progressive scholars broaden it
- **Example**: Interest-based economics in countries without alternatives

### Minority Fiqh

- **Issue**: How should Muslims in non-Muslim countries apply Islamic law?
- **Positions**: Some permit relaxation; others insist on full compliance
- **Example**: Practicing Islamic law in Western legal systems

## Practical Application in Daily Life

### Seeking Fatwas

A **fatwa** is a scholarly legal ruling. Muslims may seek fatwa from trusted scholars on various matters:
- Marriage and divorce matters
- Business transactions
- Medical decisions
- Ritual practices

### Finding Guidance

- **Fiqh books**: References for different schools
- **Online fatwa portals**: Quick consultation (use reputable sources)
- **Imam consultations**: Local mosque leaders
- **Scholarly institutions**: Al-Azhar, Islamic University of Medina, etc.

## Comparative Islamic Law

Islamic jurisprudence shares principles with other legal systems:

- **Common law**: Precedent (analogous to earlier rulings)
- **Civil law**: Written code (the Quran and codified Islamic law)
- **Natural law**: Inherent human rights in Islamic context
- **Religious law**: Similar to Jewish Halakha and Christian canon law

Understanding these comparisons helps Muslims appreciate the depth and flexibility of Islamic jurisprudence while maintaining its unique principles.
