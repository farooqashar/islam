# Aqeedah: Islamic Theology and Creed

> "Say, 'He is Allah, [who is] One, Allah, the Eternal Refuge. He neither begets nor is born, nor is there to Him any equivalent.'" -- Quran 112:1-4 (Surah Al-Ikhlas)

Aqeedah (also spelled Aqida) refers to Islamic beliefs or creed—the fundamental theological principles upon which Islam is built. It encompasses beliefs in Allah's oneness, His attributes, prophethood, the Hereafter, and divine justice.

## The Central Principle: Tawhid (Divine Unity)

Tawhid is the most fundamental concept in Islam and the basis of all Aqeedah. The word literally means "making one" but theologically means affirming the absolute oneness and uniqueness of Allah.

### Three Aspects of Tawhid

#### 1. Tawhid ar-Rububiyyah (Lordship)
The belief that Allah alone is the Creator, Sustainer, and Controller of all creation. No one creates, sustains, or governs but Allah.

- **Allah is the sole Creator**: "Is there any creator other than Allah who provides for you from the heaven and earth?" (Quran 35:3)
- **Allah sustains creation**: Everything depends on Allah for existence and continuation
- **Allah controls all affairs**: From cosmic movements to personal matters, all is in Allah's hands

#### 2. Tawhid al-Uluhiyyah (Worship)
The belief that Allah alone is worthy of worship and obedience. No one deserves worship or service except Allah.

- **No god but Allah**: La ilaha illallah—this is the core Islamic statement
- **Exclusive devotion**: All acts of worship belong to Allah alone
- **Obedience to Allah**: Following Allah's commands as an expression of worship
- **Rejecting idolatry**: Refusing to ascribe divine qualities or power to created beings

#### 3. Tawhid al-Asma wa-Sifat (Divine Names and Attributes)
The belief in Allah's names and attributes as revealed in the Quran and Sunnah, avoiding false conceptions about Allah's nature.

**The 99 Names of Allah (Asma al-Husna)**:
- **Al-Qahhar** (The Mighty): Unstoppable in power
- **As-Salaam** (The Peace): Source of tranquility and safety
- **Al-'Alim** (The All-Knowing): Omniscient
- **Al-Qawi** (The Strong): Almighty in strength
- **Ar-Rahman** (The Merciful): Full of compassion
- **Ar-Rahim** (The Compassionate): Merciful in action
- **Al-Adl** (The Just): Perfectly just in all dealings
- **Al-Hakim** (The Wise): Perfect wisdom in all affairs

### Shirk (Polytheism and Idolatry)

The opposite of Tawhid is Shirk—associating partners with Allah in worship, attributes, or actions.

**Types of Shirk**:

- **Shirk al-Akbar (Major Shirk)**: 
  - Worshipping idols, saints, or other beings
  - Invoking anyone besides Allah for help
  - Prostrating to anyone but Allah
  - Swearing oaths to anyone but Allah
  - This leads to permanent disbelief if committed

- **Shirk al-Asghar (Minor Shirk)**:
  - Showing off in worship (riya')
  - Subtle forms of self-worship or arrogance
  - This doesn't remove one from Islam but is a grave sin

**Historical Context**: Pre-Islamic Arabia was filled with Shirk—the Ka'bah housed 360 idols, and people invoked saints and angels for intercession.

## Attributes of Allah (Sifat Allah)

Classical Islamic theology developed systematic understanding of Allah's attributes. These are derived from the Quran and Sunnah.

### The Eternal Attributes

Islamic theologians affirm that Allah's attributes are:

1. **Eternal (Azali)**: They have no beginning
2. **Everlasting (Abadi)**: They have no end
3. **Not created**: They are essential to Allah's being
4. **Unchanging**: They are not subject to time or change
5. **Essential to Allah's perfection**: They describe His essence and actions

### Classification of Attributes

#### Attributes of Essence (Sifat adh-Dhaat)

Attributes essential to Allah's being:
- **Existence (Wujud)**: Allah exists necessarily; He is eternal
- **Oneness (Wahda)**: Allah is one and unique
- **Incomparability (Mukhalafah lilkhalq)**: Utterly different from creation
- **Immortality (Baqa)**: Eternal and unchanging existence

#### Attributes of Action (Sifat al-Af'al)

Attributes relating to Allah's actions:
- **Creating (Khalq)**: Allah alone creates all things
- **Sustaining (Ruzq)**: Allah provides for all creation
- **Causing death (Imata)**: Allah takes souls when their term is complete
- **Resurrection (Ihya)**: Allah brings creatures back to life on the Day of Judgment
- **Willing (Mashiyyah)**: Allah's absolute will determines all affairs
- **Power (Qudrah)**: Allah has infinite power over all things

#### Attributes of Perfection (Sifat al-Kamal)

Attributes describing Allah's perfect nature:
- **Knowledge ('Ilm)**: Allah's complete and eternal knowledge of all things
- **Power (Qudrah)**: Infinite omnipotence
- **Mercy (Rahmah)**: Boundless compassion
- **Justice (Adl)**: Perfect fairness and equity
- **Wisdom (Hikmah)**: Perfect wisdom in all divine actions
- **Hearing (Sam')**: Perfect perception of all sounds
- **Sight (Basar)**: Perfect perception of all sights
- **Life (Hayah)**: Perfect and eternal existence

### The Impossible Attributes (Mustahilat)

Attributes that cannot apply to Allah:

- **Ignorance**: Allah is all-knowing
- **Powerlessness**: Allah is all-powerful
- **Injustice**: Allah is absolutely just
- **Need**: Allah is independent; creation needs Him
- **Change**: Allah is unchanging
- **Death**: Allah is eternal
- **Embodiment**: Allah has no physical body
- **Weakness**: Allah is supremely powerful
- **Deception or dishonesty**: Allah is truthful
- **Sleep or fatigue**: Allah sustains creation without rest

## Vision of Allah (Ru'yat Allah)

A significant theological debate concerns whether believers will see Allah in Paradise.

### Sunni Position
- **Quranic basis**: "Some faces, that Day, will be radiant, looking at their Lord" (Quran 75:22-23)
- **Affirmation**: Believers will see Allah in Paradise without asking "how" (bila kayf)
- **Not corporeal**: The vision is not physical sight with eyes, but a perception suited to Paradise
- **Ultimate reward**: Seeing Allah surpasses all other pleasures of Paradise

### Mu'tazilite Position (Minority)
- **Argument**: Allah is unlike creation; vision implies form and place
- **Reasoning**: This is logically impossible given Allah's transcendence
- **Quranic interpretation**: "No vision can grasp Him, but His grasp is over all vision" (Quran 6:103) means no vision at all

### Traditional Scholars' Approach
Most classical scholars avoided definitive answers, saying: "Affirm what Allah has stated and leave the particulars to Allah" (Aqidatu Tahawiyyah approach).

## Divine Will and Human Free Will (Al-Qadar wa-Al-Ikhtiyar)

One of the most complex theological questions in Islam: How do Allah's absolute foreknowledge and decree reconcile with human free choice?

### The Quranic Framework

The Quran affirms both:
- **Divine decree**: "Indeed, all things We created with predestination" (Quran 54:49)
- **Human choice**: "There is no compulsion in religion" (Quran 2:256)
- **Responsibility**: "No soul bears a burden other than that which it carries" (Quran 17:15)

### Schools of Thought

#### Jabriyyah (Compulsion)
- **Position**: Humans have no free will; Allah predetermines all actions
- **Problem**: Makes divine punishment seem unjust
- **Status**: Rejected by mainstream Islamic scholars

#### Mu'tazilah (Rationalism)
- **Position**: Humans have complete free will independent of Allah's will
- **Problem**: Seemingly limits Allah's power and knowledge
- **Status**: Influenced theology but considered extreme by many

#### Ash'arite/Maturidite Balance (Mainstream)
- **Concept**: Divine will and human choice both operate, but in different domains
- **Kasb (Acquisition)**: Humans acquire actions they choose; Allah creates all things including actions
- **Divine knowledge**: Allah knows what humans will freely choose
- **Responsibility**: Humans are responsible for chosen actions while Allah determines outcomes
- **Analogy**: Like a parent knowing a child will choose candy from a selection but not forcing the choice

#### Maturidite Emphasis
- Greater emphasis on human choice and responsibility
- Allah's knowledge encompasses all possibilities
- Humans have genuine agency within Allah's overall plan

## Prophethood (An-Nubuwwah)

The belief in prophets and messengers is fundamental to Islamic Aqeedah.

### Definition and Necessity

**Prophet (Nabi)**: One who receives revelation from Allah
**Messenger (Rasul)**: One who brings a new message and legislation

**Why prophethood is necessary** (according to Islamic reasoning):
- Humans cannot achieve perfection without divine guidance
- Allah's mercy requires sending guidance through prophets
- Prophets provide practical examples of following Allah's guidance

### Characteristics of Prophets

Islamic theology affirms that prophets possess specific qualities:

1. **Infallibility in conveying revelation ('Isma)**: Prophets accurately convey what Allah revealed
2. **Truthfulness (Sidq)**: Cannot lie or deceive about their message
3. **Trustworthiness (Amanah)**: Faithful to their mission
4. **Intelligence ('Aql)**: Possess high intelligence to understand and teach
5. **Physical soundness**: Generally healthy to fulfill their mission
   - **Exception**: Some scholars allow prophets to have non-communicable disabilities

### Prophets are Not Divine

Important Islamic distinctions:
- **Not gods**: Prophets are created servants of Allah
- **Not infallible in personal affairs**: They can make mistakes in worldly matters
- **Not infallible in religious rulings**: Before revelation, they might err in judgment
- **Subject to death**: Prophets die like other humans
- **Needing sustenance**: Prophets eat, drink, and have bodily needs

### The Seal of Prophethood

**Muhammad is the final prophet**:
- **Quranic basis**: "Muhammad is not the father of any of your men, but [is] the Messenger of Allah and last of the prophets" (Quran 33:40)
- **Implications**: No prophet will come after Muhammad
- **Previous prophets**: All earlier prophets preached Islam in their context
- **Universality**: Muhammad's message is for all humanity and all times

## The Hereafter (Al-Akhirah)

Islamic Aqeedah includes detailed beliefs about the life after death.

### Stages of the Hereafter

#### 1. Death and the Barzakh
- **The soul leaves the body**: "It is He who takes your souls by night and has knowledge of all that you have done by day" (Quran 6:60)
- **Intermediate state**: Between death and resurrection (Barzakh)
- **Questioning in the grave**: Two angels (Munkar and Nakir) question the deceased about faith
- **State of the soul**: The soul remains conscious; believers experience ease, disbelievers experience torment
- **Duration**: Until the Day of Resurrection

#### 2. The Day of Resurrection (Yawm al-Qiyamah)
- **First Trumpet**: Israfil blows the trumpet; all creation dies
- **Second Trumpet**: All souls are resurrected for judgment
- **Bodily resurrection**: Bodies and souls reunite
- **Questioning**: Each soul questioned about deeds

#### 3. The Day of Judgment (Yawm ad-Deen)
- **Gathering**: All humanity assembled; sun brought near (Quran 84:9)
- **Standing before Allah**: Awaiting judgment
- **Weighing of deeds**: "The Day when We will bring out for them a record which speaks the truth" (Quran 17:13)
- **Intercession**: Prophets intercede; believers benefit from Prophet Muhammad's intercession
- **Separation**: Believers and disbelievers separated

#### 4. Paradise (Al-Jannah)

**Eight Gates of Paradise**: Different entrances for those who excelled in different deeds
- **Bab as-Salah**: For those diligent in prayer
- **Bab al-Jihad**: For those who struggled in Allah's cause
- **Bab as-Sadaqah**: For the charitable
- **Bab ar-Rayyan**: For those who fasted

**Levels of Paradise**:
- **Jannah ad-Daraja**: Highest level; reserved for the prophets and most righteous
- **Jannah an-Na'im**: Gardens of bliss for believers
- **Jannah al-Adan**: Gardens of eternity

**Descriptions of Paradise**:
- Rivers of water, milk, honey, and wine (non-intoxicating)
- Fruits that don't spoil; endless pleasures
- Palaces and gardens beyond imagination
- Reunion with loved ones
- No pain, sorrow, or suffering
- Close proximity to Allah (but no vision for some scholars)
- **Greatest reward**: "Their Lord has prepared for them gardens beneath which rivers flow, to stay therein. That is the great attainment" (Quran 9:72)

#### 5. Hellfire (An-Nar)

**Levels of Hellfire**:
- **Jahannam**: First and least severe level
- **Laza**: More severe
- **As-Sa'ir**: More severe still
- **Saqar**: Very severe
- **Hawiyah**: Bottommost level for those who rejected Allah

**Characteristics of Hellfire**:
- Fire hotter than earthly fire
- Chains and shackles
- Guardians (angels) administering punishment
- Everlasting for disbelievers: "Those who disbelieve—theirs will be the fire of Hell. Neither will it have finished with them so they can die, nor will its torment be lightened for them" (Quran 35:36)
- **Temporary for some believers**: Those who believed but committed major sins; eventually enter Paradise after punishment

**Important Islamic belief**:
- Eternal hellfire only for those who died in disbelief
- Major sinners among believers will eventually enter Paradise
- Mercy and justice balanced in Islamic eschatology

#### 6. Al-A'raf (The Barrier)

A middle state between Paradise and Hellfire for those whose good and bad deeds are equal:
- "And between them will be a partition, and on [its] elevations are people who recognize all by their appearance" (Quran 7:46)
- Different interpretations exist about their ultimate destination

## Divine Justice ('Adl)

Islamic theology emphasizes that Allah is absolutely just and cannot commit injustice.

### Principles of Divine Justice

1. **No punishment without law**: "We never punish until We have sent a messenger" (Quran 17:15)
2. **Clear communication**: Allah sends clear signs and messengers to all peoples
3. **Accountability for knowledge**: People held accountable for what they know
4. **Mercy for the ignorant**: Those in darkness without knowledge face lesser punishment
5. **Capability before obligation**: "Allah does not burden any soul beyond that which it can bear" (Quran 2:286)

### The Problem of Evil

How does Allah's justice coexist with suffering in the world?

**Islamic responses**:
- **Tests**: Suffering tests faith and purifies the soul
- **Consequences**: Some suffering results from natural laws and human choices
- **Wisdom**: Allah's wisdom may be beyond human comprehension
- **Temporary**: Worldly suffering is temporary; eternity is eternal
- **Compensation**: Believers' suffering is recorded and rewarded in the Hereafter
- **Mercy**: Overall, divine mercy encompasses all things

## Predestination and Responsibility (Al-Qadar)

Islamic theology affirms:
- **Allah's absolute knowledge**: All things known from eternity
- **Allah's absolute decree**: All things occur by Allah's will
- **Human choice**: Humans genuinely choose their actions
- **Divine justice**: Punishment is just because humans freely choose sins

The Quranic approach: Don't speculate excessively on this mysterious topic ("If you go deep into this matter, you will be destroyed").

## Schools of Islamic Theology (Madhahib al-Aqeedah)

### Ash'arite School
- **Founder**: Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari (9th-10th century)
- **Approach**: Combines reason and revelation; rational defense of Quranic teachings
- **Key concepts**: Kasb (acquisition of actions); divine will and human choice
- **Region**: Widespread across Islamic world
- **Character**: Orthodox position; rejects Mu'tazilite rationalism while using rational methods

### Maturidite School
- **Founder**: Abu Mansur al-Maturidi (9th-10th century)
- **Approach**: Similar to Ash'arite but greater emphasis on human reason and responsibility
- **Difference**: Greater role for human intellect and choice
- **Region**: Central Asia; strong in Turkey, Pakistan
- **Character**: Orthodox but with more rationalist flavor

### Athari School
- **Approach**: Literal affirmation of Quranic and Hadith texts without rational interpretation
- **Method**: "Affirm without asking 'how'" (Bila kayf)
- **Founders**: Ibn Hanbal, Ibn Taymiyyah
- **Region**: Saudi Arabia, some Salafi movements
- **Character**: Conservative; rejects speculative theology

### Maturidite-Ash'arite Synthesis

Most contemporary Islamic institutions synthesize these approaches:
- Affirm Quranic and Hadith texts directly
- Use rational arguments to defend Islamic beliefs
- Balance reason and revelation
- Avoid excessive speculation

## Modern Theological Challenges

### Compatibility with Modern Science

**Issues**:
- Evolution and the creation of humans
- Medical interventions and divine will
- Astronomical discoveries and Quranic cosmology

**Islamic responses**:
- Theistic evolution compatible with Quranic teaching
- Allah works through natural causes and laws
- Quranic verses describe religious, not scientific, truths
- Science describes "how"; religion answers "why"

### Gender and Divine Justice

**Issue**: Why does Islamic law differentiate between men and women in some areas?

**Islamic responses**:
- Gender differentiation based on different roles and capabilities
- Justice means equity, not uniformity
- Women's rights protected in many areas (property, divorce initiation, inheritance)
- Reexamination of traditional interpretations in light of Quranic principles

### Religious Pluralism

**Issue**: How can Islam claim truth while respecting other religions?

**Islamic teaching**:
- Quran recognizes truth in previous scriptures (before corruption)
- Different communities have different commandments (5:48)
- "No compulsion in religion" (2:256)
- "To you your religion, and to me mine" (109:6)
- Judgment belongs to Allah; humans should show respect and compassion

## Living Islamic Aqeedah

### Practical Implications

Islamic Aqeedah isn't merely intellectual but shapes how Muslims live:

- **Trust in Allah (Tawakkul)**: Belief in divine care leads to reliance on Allah
- **Patience (Sabr)**: Knowing Allah controls outcomes brings patience in hardship
- **Justice and mercy**: Belief in Allah's justice motivates just treatment of others
- **Humility**: Recognizing Allah's majesty humbles the soul
- **Motivation for good deeds**: Belief in accountability encourages righteous behavior
- **Hope and fear balance**: Neither despair nor false security; balanced approach

### Strengthening Aqeedah

Ways to deepen Islamic beliefs:
- **Reflection (Tafakkur)**: Contemplating the signs of Allah in creation
- **Learning**: Studying Islamic sciences and theolog
- **Supplication**: Asking Allah to strengthen one's conviction
- **Righteous actions**: Deeds reinforce belief
- **Community**: Learning from and with other believers
- **Spiritual discipline**: Regular worship and remembrance of Allah

Islamic Aqeedah provides a comprehensive framework for understanding reality, morality, and one's place in creation—a framework that has sustained Islamic civilization and continues to guide hundreds of millions of Muslims worldwide.
