# Islamic Finance and Economics

## Foundations

Islamic finance is rooted in Shariah principles derived from the Quran and Sunnah. The core goal is to ensure economic activity is ethical, equitable, and serves the common good.

- "Allah has permitted trade and prohibited riba" (2:275)
- "Do not consume one another's wealth unjustly" (2:188)
- Wealth is a trust (amanah) from God, not an absolute right

## Core Prohibitions

### Riba (Interest/Usury)

The most fundamental prohibition. Covers both interest on loans (Riba al-Nasiah) and unequal exchange of same commodity (Riba al-Fadl). The Prophet cursed the one who takes riba, the one who pays it, the one who records it, and the witnesses (Sahih Muslim 1598).

### Gharar (Excessive Uncertainty)

Contracts with excessive ambiguity are prohibited. Examples: selling something you do not own, selling fish still in the sea.

### Maysir (Gambling)

All forms of gambling and speculative games of chance are prohibited (5:90).

### Haram Industries

Investment in alcohol, pork, pornography, conventional interest-based banking, and weapons of mass destruction is prohibited.

## Permissible Financial Contracts

- **Murabaha** (Cost-Plus Financing): bank buys asset, sells to client at disclosed markup
- **Mudarabah** (Profit-Sharing): one provides capital, other provides labor; profits shared by ratio
- **Musharakah** (Partnership): all partners contribute capital, share profits/losses
- **Ijarah** (Leasing): bank buys and leases asset to client
- **Salam** (Forward Sale): full payment upfront for future delivery
- **Istisna** (Manufacturing Contract): contract to build to specification
- **Sukuk** (Islamic Bonds): asset-backed securities; global market exceeded $800 billion by the 2020s

## Takaful (Islamic Insurance)

Participants contribute to a mutual pool based on cooperation (ta'awun) and donation (tabarru), avoiding the gharar of conventional insurance.

## The Modern Islamic Finance Industry

- Approximately **$4 trillion** in total assets by the mid-2020s
- Major centers: Malaysia, Saudi Arabia, UAE, Bahrain, Qatar, Indonesia, Turkey, UK
- **AAOIFI** and **IFSB** set industry standards
- Every major conventional bank now has an Islamic finance window

## Criticisms and Debates

- Some argue products like murabaha are economically identical to interest-bearing loans
- Debate on cryptocurrency: rulings vary by jurisdiction
- Industry evolving toward more equity-based (musharakah/mudarabah) models
