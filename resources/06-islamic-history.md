# Islamic History

## The Rashidun Caliphate (632-661 CE / 11-41 AH)

The four "Rightly-Guided" caliphs who succeeded Prophet Muhammad:

### Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (632-634 CE)

First caliph. Fought the Riddah (apostasy) Wars to reunify Arabia. Ordered the first compilation of the Quran into a single manuscript. Launched expeditions into Syria and Iraq.

### Umar ibn al-Khattab (634-644 CE)

Second caliph. Oversaw massive expansion: conquest of Jerusalem (637), Egypt (641), and the Sassanid Persian Empire. Administrative innovations include the diwan (registry) system, the Islamic calendar (Hijri), and Bayt al-Mal (public treasury). Assassinated by a Persian slave.

### Uthman ibn Affan (644-656 CE)

Third caliph. Standardized the Quran into the Uthmani codex. Continued territorial expansion and built the first Muslim navy. Faced growing internal dissent and was assassinated during a siege of his home.

### Ali ibn Abi Talib (656-661 CE)

Fourth caliph, also the Prophet's cousin and son-in-law. His caliphate was marked by the First Fitna (civil war): the Battle of the Camel (656) against Aisha, Talha, and Zubayr; and the Battle of Siffin (657) against Muawiyah. The Kharijites emerged from a dispute over arbitration. Ali was assassinated by a Kharijite in 661.

## The Umayyad Caliphate (661-750 CE / 41-132 AH)

- **Muawiyah I** established dynastic rule from Damascus
- **The Tragedy of Karbala (680 CE)**: Husayn ibn Ali (the Prophet's grandson) was killed by Umayyad forces, a defining event for Shi'a Islam
- **Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan**: Arabized the administration, built the Dome of the Rock in Jerusalem (691 CE)
- **Al-Walid I**: built the Great Mosque of Damascus; Muslim forces reached Spain (711) and Sindh (712)
- **Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz** (r. 717-720): known as the "fifth rightly-guided caliph" for his piety and justice
- **Battle of Tours/Poitiers (732)**: halted Muslim advance into Western Europe
- The Abbasid revolution overthrew the Umayyads in 750; a surviving prince established an emirate in Al-Andalus (Spain)

## The Abbasid Caliphate (750-1258 CE / 132-656 AH)

- **Al-Mansur** founded Baghdad (762 CE) as the new capital
- **Harun al-Rashid** (r. 786-809): golden era, diplomatic exchanges with Charlemagne
- **Al-Ma'mun** (r. 813-833): established Bayt al-Hikmah (House of Wisdom) for translation and scholarship; imposed the Mihna (inquisition) over the created Quran doctrine

### The Islamic Golden Age

Major scholars and achievements during the Abbasid period:

- **Al-Khwarizmi** (d. ~850): algebra, algorithms
- **Ibn al-Haytham** (d. ~1040): optics, scientific method
- **Al-Razi** (d. ~925): clinical medicine, chemistry
- **Ibn Sina / Avicenna** (d. 1037): Canon of Medicine, philosophy
- **Al-Biruni** (d. ~1048): geography, astronomy, comparative religion
- **Ibn Khaldun** (d. 1406): historiography, sociology (Muqaddimah)

Political fragmentation saw the rise of independent dynasties: Fatimids in Egypt (909-1171), Buyids in Baghdad, Seljuk Turks (who championed Sunni revival). The Mongol sack of Baghdad in 1258 ended the Abbasid Caliphate, though a symbolic line continued in Mamluk Cairo.

## Al-Andalus (711-1492)

Tariq ibn Ziyad crossed into Iberia in 711. The Umayyad Emirate (756) and later Caliphate of Cordoba (929) presided over a flourishing civilization. **Convivencia** describes the coexistence of Muslims, Christians, and Jews.

Notable figures: **Ibn Rushd / Averroes** (philosophy), **Ibn Arabi** (mysticism), **Abbas ibn Firnas** (aviation pioneer). Cordoba's library held over 400,000 volumes.

The Reconquista gradually reclaimed territory. The Taifa kingdoms fractured Muslim rule; the Almoravids and Almohads provided temporary reinforcement. Granada, the last Muslim state, fell in 1492.

## The Crusades (1095-1291)

- **First Crusade (1099)**: Crusaders captured Jerusalem, established Crusader states
- **Salah al-Din (Saladin)**: defeated Crusaders at Battle of Hattin (1187), recaptured Jerusalem
- **Third Crusade**: Richard I negotiated access for Christian pilgrims
- **Mamluks** defeated the Mongols at Ain Jalut (1260) and expelled the last Crusaders from Acre (1291)

## The Mongol Period

Genghis Khan's campaigns devastated the Khwarazmian Empire (1219-1221). Hulagu Khan sacked Baghdad in 1258, killing the last Abbasid caliph. The Mamluk victory at Ain Jalut (1260) halted Mongol westward expansion. The Ilkhanate later converted to Islam. Timur (Tamerlane, d. 1405) caused further destruction across the Muslim world.

## The Ottoman Empire (c. 1299-1922)

- **Rise**: Osman I founded the dynasty; rapid expansion in Anatolia and the Balkans
- **Mehmed II** conquered Constantinople in 1453, renaming it Istanbul
- **Selim I** conquered Egypt and the Levant, claiming the caliphate
- **Suleiman the Magnificent** (r. 1520-1566): empire at its peak; legal reforms (Kanuni), siege of Vienna (1529)
- **Millet system**: granted religious communities internal autonomy
- **Decline**: military defeats, territorial losses, the "Sick Man of Europe"
- **Tanzimat reforms** (1839-1876): modernization efforts
- **WWI**: Ottoman alliance with Central Powers, Gallipoli, Arab Revolt
- **Dissolution**: Turkish Republic declared (1923), caliphate abolished (1924)

## The Safavid Empire (1501-1736)

- **Shah Ismail I** established Twelver Shi'ism as Iran's state religion
- **Shah Abbas I the Great** (r. 1588-1629): Isfahan became a magnificent capital, military and economic reforms
- Rivalry with the Ottomans defined the Sunni-Shi'a geopolitical divide
- Battle of Chaldiran (1514): Ottoman victory limited Safavid expansion

## The Mughal Empire (1526-1857)

- **Babur** won the First Battle of Panipat (1526)
- **Akbar** (r. 1556-1605): religious tolerance, administrative reforms, Din-i Ilahi experiment
- **Shah Jahan** (r. 1628-1658): built the Taj Mahal and Red Fort
- **Aurangzeb** (r. 1658-1707): maximum territorial extent, more orthodox policies
- Decline led to British East India Company dominance; the Mughal dynasty formally ended after the 1857 Sepoy Mutiny

## Colonial Period and Independence

European powers colonized most of the Muslim world in the 19th-20th centuries:

- British: India, Egypt, Malaya, parts of the Gulf and East Africa
- French: North Africa (Algeria, Tunisia, Morocco), Levant (Syria, Lebanon)
- Dutch: Indonesia
- Russian: Central Asia and the Caucasus

Reform movements emerged: **Jamal al-Din al-Afghani** (Pan-Islamism), **Muhammad Abduh** (Islamic modernism), **Rashid Rida**, the **Deobandi** and **Aligarh** movements in South Asia, the **Wahhabi** movement in Arabia.

Key events: abolition of the Ottoman Caliphate (1924), creation of Saudi Arabia (1932), independence of Pakistan (1947), Indonesian independence (1945), Algerian War of Independence (1954-1962), Iranian Revolution (1979).

## Contemporary Period

- Organization of Islamic Cooperation (OIC) founded 1969
- Modern Islamist movements: Muslim Brotherhood (est. 1928), Jamaat-e-Islami
- Gulf states rapid development from oil wealth
- Bosnian War (1992-1995) and genocide against Bosnian Muslims
- September 11, 2001 and its global aftermath
- Arab Spring (2011) and its consequences
- Growing Muslim diaspora communities in Europe, North America, and Australia
- Approximately 1.9 billion Muslims worldwide as of 2024, about 24% of the global population
